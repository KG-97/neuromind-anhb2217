Section 01

Gross anatomy

The foundation. Examiners love dressing these basics in ugly wording. Do not let them.

Vertebral segments are the bony vertebrae. Spinal segments are neural cord segments that give rise to spinal nerves.

Lower spinal segments do not line up with the same-numbered vertebrae because the vertebral column outgrows the spinal cord.

The cord ends around L1–L2, but lower nerves still need to exit through lower foramina, so lumbar and sacral roots descend farther within the canal.

Cauda equina

Bundle of elongated lumbar, sacral, and coccygeal roots below the conus.

Important for lumbar puncture: roots float in CSF and can move away from the needle.

Filum terminale

Fibrous pia extension anchoring the cord to the coccyx.

It tethers the cord longitudinally so it does not slosh around like an unsecured cable.

Dorsal rootlets

Enter at the posterolateral sulcus.

Afferent sensory fibres.

Ventral rootlets

Exit at the anterolateral sulcus.

Efferent motor fibres.

The dural and subarachnoid sac extends to about S2, well below the cord ending at L1–L2. That is why lumbar puncture at L3–L4 or L4–L5 can access CSF without spearing the cord itself.

Section 02

Segment characteristics

White matter dominates superiorly because fibres accumulate as they ascend. Grey matter gains ground inferiorly.

Level clues

Cervical

Oval shape, high white matter, large ventral horns in enlargement levels.

Thoracic

Smaller cord with lateral horns for sympathetic outflow.

Lumbar

Rounder with large dorsal and ventral horns for lower limb function.

Sacral

Grey-dominant with parasympathetic outflow from S2–S4.

Section 03

Roots, ganglia, and rami

Roots are functionally pure. Spinal nerves and rami are mixed. Mix that up and your marks go missing.

AfferentSensoryDRG soma

Dorsal components

Dorsal root ganglia contain pseudounipolar sensory neuron cell bodies.

Dorsal rami supply intrinsic back muscles and overlying skin.

EfferentMotorVentral horn soma

Ventral components

Alpha motor neurons drive extrafusal muscle; gamma motor neurons adjust spindle sensitivity.

Ventral rami supply limbs and ventrolateral trunk and form plexuses.

Rule worth memorising

After dorsal and ventral roots unite, the spinal nerve is mixed.

Second rule worth memorising

After the spinal nerve splits, both dorsal and ventral rami are still mixed.

Section 04

Spinal tracts

Know the modality, know where it crosses, and half the lesion question is already dead.

AscendingFine touchVibrationProprioception

1st order

Receptor → DRG → ascends ipsilaterally in dorsal columns.

Gracilis below T6, cuneatus above T6.

2nd order

Synapses in nucleus gracilis/cuneatus in the medulla and crosses as internal arcuate fibres.

3rd order

VPL thalamus → primary somatosensory cortex.

Dorsal column lesion in the spinal cord causes ipsilateral loss of vibration, fine touch, and proprioception below the lesion.

Clinical pearl

In the cord, DCML findings are ipsilateral because crossing happens in the medulla, not the spinal cord.

AscendingPainTemperatureCrude touch

1st order

Nociceptor/thermoreceptor → DRG → dorsal horn.

2nd order

Starts in dorsal horn and crosses in the anterior white commissure within 1–2 segments.

3rd order

Ascends contralaterally to VPL thalamus, then S1.

Central cord lesions such as syringomyelia can damage crossing fibres and produce bilateral segmental pain/temperature loss.

Clinical pearl

Spinal cord hemisection usually causes contralateral pain and temperature loss below the lesion.

DescendingVoluntary movementUMN → LMN

Lateral CST

~85% of fibres cross at the pyramidal decussation in the medulla and control distal limb movement.

Anterior CST

More axial/proximal bias; many fibres cross near their spinal level of termination.

LMN endpoint

Upper motor neurons synapse on ventral horn lower motor neurons, which exit via ventral roots.

UMN vs LMN

UMN: spasticity, hyperreflexia, clonus, Babinski.

LMN: flaccidity, atrophy, fasciculations, hyporeflexia.

Section 05

Spinal reflexes

Fast, local, and absolutely uninterested in waiting for cortical permission.

Monosynaptic stretch reflex

Ia afferent from a muscle spindle synapses directly onto an alpha motor neuron.

Fastest reflex loop. Example: patellar reflex.

Polysynaptic reflex

Uses interneurons, allowing coordinated activation of agonists and inhibition of antagonists.

Reciprocal inhibition stops the antagonist from sabotaging the movement.

Pain withdrawal reflex

1 · Stimulus

Pain activates nociceptors. Aδ fibres carry fast sharp pain; C fibres slower burning pain.

2 · Afferent limb

Signal travels to the DRG, enters through the dorsal root, and synapses in the dorsal horn.

3 · Integration

Interneurons excite flexors and inhibit antagonistic extensors.

4 · Efferent limb

Alpha motor neurons in the ventral horn fire and exit via the ventral root.

5 · Response

The limb withdraws while ascending pathways still tell the brain you did something regrettable.
